CURB · Press kit

SF's posted 2-hour street-cleaning window is really ~20 minutes.

CURB is a free map of San Francisco street-parking rules — and the only one that shows when tickets actually get written, rebuilt from over a million GPS-located SFMTA citations. Tap your curb, know the rule, move your car in time.

The story

San Francisco posts a two-hour street-cleaning window on every block — but the ticket doesn't wait two hours. Alejandro Vizio, a product designer in the Haight, obtained the city's complete street-cleaning citation record — about a million GPS-located tickets, via a public-records request — and matched each to the exact block it was written on. The finding: on a typical block, the tickets land in a tight ~15-minute burst right after the window opens, a median of 25 minutes in; citywide, ~90% are written within the first hour. The posted "two hours" is mostly decorative.

He built CURB in about a month, solo — after he and his girlfriend ran up roughly $2,000 in tickets — using the city's public open data and AI (Claude) for the data analysis and backend. It's free, has no account, no ads, and no tracking, and it's open source. A free native iOS app is now on the App Store. The posted physical sign is always the final authority — CURB never overrides it.

Two findings reporters love

The 20-minute reality

Take 214–255 Steiner St (posted 9–11am). 2024–2026: 221 tickets, earliest at 9:00am sharp, the median ticket at 9:11am, and 90% already written by 9:21am. "I'll move it before 11" is exactly how you get a $105 ticket.

You can re-park after the sweeper passes

Most drivers don't know this SFMTA rule: once the sweeper has physically passed your side of the street, you can re-park — even before the posted window ends. No need to circle the block for an hour. (You still can't park before the truck arrives.)

About the maker

Alejandro Vizio is a product designer and developer in San Francisco's Haight-Ashbury. Originally from Argentina, he's lived in SF for ten years. He built CURB by himself over about a month, after racking up roughly $2,000 in parking tickets — designing the app and using AI (Claude) for the data analysis and backend. He previously built mapping tools at Pachama, a carbon-credit startup. CURB is free and open source; his goal is simply to help San Franciscans read the city's own parking rules.

Fast facts

  • Free — no account, no ads, no tracking, no data collection.
  • Open source (MIT) — github.com/alevizio/curb.
  • Built solo, in ~a month, with AI (Claude) for the backend + data.
  • Runs entirely on San Francisco public open data (DataSF).
  • Live web app at curb.guide; free native iOS app on the App Store.
  • Shows street sweeping, permit (RPP) zones, meters, color curb, and ~1,975 loading zones the city's data omits.
  • Move-your-car alerts: choose Light, Normal, or Intense — calendar or push.
  • The posted physical sign is always the final authority.
